# Pilot customer kit

A pilot customer is the one item in this package no document can substitute for —
it requires you, in rooms, with real organizations. This kit compresses the path.

## Who to target (in order of likely yes)

**Deaf services organizations and university disability offices.** Fastest yes:
they feel the interpreter-booking pain daily, they're mission-aligned, and their
endorsement is the credibility every later customer will ask about. A campus
disability services office piloting live lecture delivery is an ideal first site —
contained venue, motivated users, FERPA is manageable at pilot scale with consent.

**Transit agencies.** Best public story ("Red Line announcements in your language,
on your phone") and the demo is already themed for it. Slower procurement; enter
through the accessibility/ADA coordinator or an innovation office, not general
procurement. Many agencies have Title VI language-access obligations they're
currently meeting with static signage — you're a cheaper, better compliance answer.

**Community health clinics.** Highest willingness to pay (interpreter line items
are real money) but HIPAA means this is pilot two, not pilot one — do it after the
first site produces field metrics.

## The approach sequence

Warm path first: state or city commissions for the Deaf/HoH, university disability
services directors, and transit accessibility coordinators are all publicly listed
and mission-driven — a two-paragraph email with the demo link gets replies here at
far higher rates than any enterprise channel. Ask for 30 minutes to show the
three-phones demo; the ask at the end of that meeting is a site visit, not a
contract. Involve end users from the first meeting — for any sign-language use,
Deaf staff and users co-design or you will build the wrong thing and be told so
publicly. Pay community consultants; don't ask for free labor.

## The pilot offer (what you say)

Ninety days, one venue, free. You instrument one channel end to end (their content
in, riders' phones out), publish a live dashboard they can watch, and hand them a
monthly signed SLA report. They provide: the venue, a staff contact two hours a
week, and permission to name them (or not — anonymous case study as fallback).
Success criteria agreed in writing up front: zero advance access requests filed,
delivery success >= 99.9%, latency within class budget, and a user-satisfaction
survey run by them, not you.

## Pilot agreement outline (one page, not a contract yet)

Scope (one venue, one channel, N users) · Term (90 days, either party exits with
7 days notice) · Data (consent-based, zero-retention conversational mode, they own
their content, you own the platform telemetry, GDPR/FERPA posture stated) · Cost
(free; both parties' good-faith intent to negotiate a paid deployment if success
criteria are met) · Safety (emergency class runs shadow-mode only — observed, not
relied upon — until certified) · Publicity (mutual approval).

## What the pilot must produce

Field versions of the proof-report numbers (real network, real users, real
content), three quotable user stories, one reference customer willing to take a
call — and that is the packet that turns the OpenAI conversation from "interesting
demo" into "let's structure something."
